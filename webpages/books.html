Health & Growth

Glucose Revolution: The Life-Changing Power of Balancing Your Blood Sugar
by Jessie Inchauspe        [Goodreads Page]        [YouTube Video]

How can we improve our health?

  • Because of our modern western diet, most of us have too much glucose in our body (a.k.a., high blood sugar level), a major cause of many health problems including obesity, diabeties, heart disease, and migraine.
  • Here are ways to balance our blood sugar level:
    • Limit food with high glycemic index (sugar, juice, processed carbs).
    • When buying groceries, choose whole foods and produce over highly processed packages with unfamiliar chemical ingredients.
    • For each meal, eat vegetables and proteins before carbs. Add vinegar.
    • Go for light exercise (e.g., 20-min walk) after a meal.
    • Get enough sleep (8 hours) and manage stress levels.

by Greg McKeown        [Goodreads Page]        [YouTube Video]

How can we feel less overwhelmed?

  • Adopting an Essentialism mindset means saying no to many good things (the trivial many) so that we can say yes to a few great things (the vital few). It's a 3-step process best described by Warren Buffett's 5/25 rule:
    1. Explore: List the top 25 things we want to do. Circle the top 5.
    2. Eliminate: Never ever think about the other 20 again. They will distract us from the 5 things that matter the most.
    3. Execute: Focus all our energy to accomplish the top 5 things. Make them effortless by building habits and systems.
  • As we grow and gain new experiences over time, it's important to periodically reevaluate and adjust our priorities.

Social Relationships

Set Boundaries, Find Peace: A Guide to Reclaiming Yourself
by Nedra Glover Tawwab        [Goodreads Page]        [YouTube Video]

How can we feel more comfortable in social relationships?

  • Boundaries are guardrails and needs to make us feel safe in our relationships. It is channeled through self-love and self-respect.
  • State your boundaries assertively ("I want/need X"). Do not apologize. Do not provide a reason. Do not feel over-responsible for other's feelings.
  • If you get pushback / questioning / silence / limit-testing:
    1. Validate: "I understand you feel X"
    2. State how you feel: “When you do Y, I feel upset and disrespected.
    3. Restate boundary: "Please do not do Y again."
    4. If they refuse to comply, issue a reasonable ultimatum: "If you do Y again, I am going to do Z.” Make sure to follow through.
  • If you anticipate pushback, that’s exactly when a boundary is needed.

How can we handle conflicts more effectively?

  1. Validate our counterparts' emotions via mirroring and labeling.
    • Mirroring: paraphrase to show understanding: "So you are saying ..."
    • Labeling: articulate underlying emotions: "Sounds like you feel upset."
  2. Uncover new information via tactical silence and “no” oriented questions.
    • Tacitcal silence: Embrace silence to encourage them to keep talking.
    • "No" oriented questions: phrase questions to encourage others to say "no," then use labeling to uncover the reasons behind resistance.
  3. Seek collaboration via open-ended questions, which start with "How" or "What" (instead of "Why") and phrased in a tone of seeking advice.

When a kid throws a tantrum, what can parents do?

  1. Believing that the tantrum always has a valid emotional reason, we can uncover the root cause via open-ended questions ("How are you feeling?") and labeling ("It seems that you feel upset because of X. Is that right?")
  2. Validate the emotion: "I feel you. Dealing with X sounds really tough. I would feel the same way if it were to happen to me."
  3. Speaking up candidly about how we feel: "When you throw a tantrum, I feel overwhelmed." Use tactical silence to pause to hear response.
  4. State your boundary:"I know it's hard, but please do not throw a tantrum again. If you throw a tantrum again, I will do Y." and follow through.
  5. Invite brainstorming win-win solutions: "hmm, I wonder what we can do."

Give and Take: Why Helping Others Drives Our Success
by Adam Grant        [Goodreads Page]        [YouTube Video]

How can we feel a sense of belonging in our community?

  • 3 types of people: (1) Givers: value helping others; (2) Takers: value self-interest; (3) Matchers: value fairness in reciprocity.
  • Bad news: people who perform worst in school and career tend to be givers, because they burn out faster and get taken advantage of by takers.
  • Good news: people who perform best are also givers, because they: (1) build strong networks and reputation; (2) foster a culture of giving; (3) are great mentors and leaders; (4) gain trust via powerless communication.
  • What separates top performing givers from bottom ones? Givers at the top: set boundaries, ask for help, keep guards up around takers.
  • To create a culture of reciprocity in organization, we should appreciate “disagreeable givers" and watch out for “agreeable takers”.

Management and Organizations

The Culture Code: The Secrets of Highly Successful Groups
by Daniel Coyle        [Goodreads Page]        [YouTube Video]

How can we improve the culture of our organizations?

  • A healthy culture requires 3 conditions: safety, vulnerability, and purpose.
  • Psychological Safety means that members feel comfortable voicing disagreements without fear. Leaders build safety by being open to feedback, care about people, and establish clear expectations.
  • Vulnerability means that members feel comfortable sharing weakness & mistakes and asking for help. Leaders build trust by being vulnerable themselves, listening without judgment, and offering mentorship.
  • Purpose means that members feel motivated to work beyond earning a paycheck. Leaders instill purpose by articulating a shared mission, connecting the mission to each member's work, and celebrating successes.

How can we get big projects done more effectively?

  • Get the Right People: Choose an experienced project lead with a proven track record of success. Let the leader build their own team.
  • Clarify Objective: "What's the project goal? What does success look like?"
  • Divide and Conquer: break down large projects into small and independence steps. Focus on one step at a time.
  • Think Slow, Act Fast: During planning stage, test hypothesis with experiments and anticipate unexpected risks.
  • Reference Past Projects: Study past cases for best practices and resource expectations.

How can our teams perform more efficiently and have fewer fire drills?

  • Identify Bottlenecks by visualizing the entire work flow (Kanban).
  • Understand Root Cause of the bottlenecks. Address the root cause by redistributing workload and redesigning/automating processes.
  • Amplify feedback loop: give status updates to and ask for feedback from your stakholders and customers more frequently to quickly correct mistakes and avoid unnecessary steps.
  • Continual Learning and Experimentation: encourages teams to take calculated risks, experiment with new ideas, and learn from both successes and failures.

How can we grab the attention of our overwhelmed audience?

  • Simplify your message to help audience remember.
  • Highlight an unexpected finding to eliminate audience's boredom.
  • Use concrete examples to clarify your message.
  • Emphasize the emotional purpose of the message to make audience care.
  • Tell a story to make your message actionable.
  • The greatest challenge to communication is the curse of knowledge: Knowing an idea makes it difficult for us to share the idea with others, because we cannot imagine what it was like not to know it. Therefore, it's important to empathize from our audience's perspective.
