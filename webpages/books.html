Top 10 books read in 2021:

In the Plex
Steven Levy

A very comprehensive and entertaining story about Google.

Genius Makers
Cade Metz

An accessible history on deep learning (aka neural network, artificial intelligence), one of the most important technological breakthroughs of our generation.

The 1-Page Marketing Plan
Allan Dib

Do not try too hard to attract all customers. Identify and target customer segments that really want your product.

Bad Blood
John Carreyrou

What an incredible story! A company with outstanding products and poor marketing is a tragedy. A company with outstanding marketing and poor products is a fraud.

The Unspoken Rules
Gorick Ng

A book I will reread periodically to prepare for my career.

Quench Your Own Thirst
Jim Koch

I don't consume much alcohol, but the story in this book is so interesting that I bought a pack of Sam Adams just to experience the pioneer of American craft beer movement. Fun fact: Sam Adams is the first beer brand in the US that puts expiration date on the beer package. Jim Koch did so because he cared deeply about ensuring a great customer experience.

Will
Will Smith

Will Smith's autobiography gives readers a context to his infamous Oscar slap. The book taught me two major lessons: 1. Everyone is vulnerable. Behind the facade of this hollywood superstar lies a deeply scarred childhood and complicated life story. 2. Nothing worth pursuing comes easy. Will's successful career was paved with a maniacal attitude towards hard work and sacrifice.

The Launch Pad
Randall Stross

This book introduces the story of Y combinator, Silicon Valley's legendary startup accelerator that produces companies including Airbnb, Dropbox, Reddit, DoorDash, Instacart, and Twitch. The very early struggles of these companies shed lights to two insights: 1. Every successful start-up requires two cofounders. 2. Invest in the people, not in the idea.

Think Again
Adam Grant

The only thing that remains constant is the inevitability of change itself. Assumptions that we take for granted often change in unexpected ways. Hence, whether we are working on a project, pursuing a career path, defending a deeply held belief, or managing an organization, it is helpful to periodically take a step back and reevaluate our approaches.

Hatching Twitter
Nick Bilton

An epic and addictive story about Twitter. It's like the book version of HBO's Silicon Valley TV show.

Top 10 books read in 2020:

The Innovator's Dilemma
Clayton Christensen

Why are small companies more likely to introduce disruptive innovations than big, resource-rich companies? Christensen provides a fascinating explanation. The key insight is that at early stages, innovations with disruptive potential often begin in a niche market. Large companies, burdened by their sizes and revenue target, are less likely to devote sufficient attention to unproven technologies in a niche market.

Lead and Disrupt
Charles O'Reilly & Michael Tushman

Given Christensen's observation, what can big companies do to protect themselves against the threat of disruption? O'Reilly and Tushman presented a solution: large companies should separate their exploratory units from the exploitation units. The exploitation units refine sustaining innovations, while the exploratory units experiment with radical innovations. To achieve their respective goals, the two types of units must adopt different organizational rules and procedures.

Unlocking the Customer Value Chain: How Decoupling Drives Consumer Disruption
Thales Teixeira

This book taught me that True disruptions do not come from innovation in fancy technologies, they come from innovation in business models via decoupling of the value chain. Companies do not thrive by finding ways to crush the competitors, they thrive by finding new ways to better serve customer needs.

Misbehaving
Richard Thaler

Taking risks is a necessary step to radical innovations. Thaler shows that while an organization must take risks to survive, individual managers often avoid risks to protect their career and status. Furthermore, switching from long-term to short-term thinking causes individuals to become risk-averse. The book shows that, contrary to the assumptions of neoclassical economics, people do not always act rationally in a world of uncertainty.

The Visible Hand
Alfred Chandler Jr.

What do managers do? Why are there so many managers in today's corporations? Chandler shows that as the scale of projects increased over the last two hundred years (began with the construction of American railroads), managers became more efficient than the "invisible hand" of the free market at coordinating production and distribution processes by reducing uncertainty.

The World's Newest Profession
Christopher McKenna

What do consultants do? If corporations already have so many managers, why do they hire consultants? McKenna shows that consultants provide values in three ways: (1)They are the knowledge brokers that give client firms access to industry best practices; (2)They are the temporary workforce for client firms' strategic planning processes; (3)Their outsider status provides legitimacy for corporate strategic decisions and protects board members from personal liabilities.

The Lean Startup
Eric Ries

Since new information constantly updates our assessment of the environment, how is long-term planning ever possible? Ries warns us not to devote too much energy to crafting a "perfect" plan. Instead, we should (1) start with a minimum viable product to test the viability of the plan; (2) periodically collect objective feedback and consider modifying the plan based on new information. This lesson applys to startups, research projects, and individual career directions.

Crossing The Chasm
Geoffrey Moore

Moore observes that many startups fail because they are unable to move from the early adopter market to the mainstream market. While early adopters are attracted by the novelty of the product, the mainstream market focuses on its convenience and practicality. Moore offers two main advice for growth in the mainstream market: (1) package the product to improve user experience; (2) target a specific market segment first before expanding to other segments.

Made to Stick
Chip Heath & Dan Heath

What kinds of information sticks in people's mind? Among the six characteristics that Heath brothers described, I find "simple" and "concrete" most important. An idea is "simple" when it is stripped down to its core and generalizable across various situations. An idea is "concrete" when its presentation allows people to easily associate it with daily experience. In social science, these two characteristics are the necessary conditions for a school of thought to become influential.

Revolution in the Development of Capitalism
Mark Gould

Gould is my academic advisor at Haverford College. He is a student of Talcott Parsons, who built an elegant and generalizable theoretical framework called Structural Functionalism. I believe Gould extended Parsons' framework to a new level by introducing a theory of change. However, the Parsonian school of thought lost popularity in the late-twentieth century mainly because its ideas were presented in a very abstract way. Nevertheless, I find Parsons and Gould's work extremely insightful.
