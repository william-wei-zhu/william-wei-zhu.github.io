William's All-Time Favorite Books

Listening to audiobooks has always been my favorite pastime. When I was 7 years old, my father began encouraging me to listen to audiobooks as a hobby. Growing up, my favorite book genres have changed from fantasy and detective novels (before college), to social and economic history (during college), and to personal growth, organizational management, and business biography (after college). My ability to digest audiobooks while multitasking (e.g. cooking and washing dishes) has become my greatest superpower.

In recent conversations, my friends and colleagues often asked me to recommend books. Therefore, I have compiled a list of my all-time favorite books organized by topics, curated from 300+ books that I finished over the past 4 years. Enjoy!

For more book recommendations, feel free to check out my Goodreads bookshelf. (The books that I rated as 4 and 5 stars are always worth a read).

Health & Growth

Give and Take: Why Helping Others Drives Our Success
by Adam Grant          [Goodreads Page]        [YouTube Video]

  • 3 types of people: (1) Givers: value helping others; (2) Takers: value self-interest; (3) Matchers: value fairness in reciprocity.
  • Bad news: people who perform worst in school and career tend to be givers, because they burn out faster and get taken advantage of by takers.
  • Good news: people who perform best are also givers, because they: (1) build strong networks and reputation; (2) foster a culture of giving around them; (3) are great mentors; (4) use powerless communication to gain trust.
  • What separates top performing givers from bottom ones? Givers at the top: set boundaries, ask for help, keep guards up around takers.
  • To create a culture of reciprocity in organization, we should appreciate “disagreeable givers" and watch out for “agreeable takers”.

Social Relationships

The Book of Boundaries: Set the Limits That Will Set You Free
by Melissa Urban        [Goodreads Page]        [YouTube Video]

A FBI hostage negotiator breaks down effective persuasion in 3 essential steps:

  1. Validate our counterparts' emotions via mirroring and labeling.
    • Mirroring: paraphrase to show understanding: "So you are saying ..."
    • Labeling: articulate underlying emotions: "Sounds like you feel upset."
  2. Uncover new information via tactical silence and “no” oriented questions.
    • Tacitcal silence: Embrace silence to encourage them to keep talking.
    • "No" oriented questions: phrase questions to encourage others to say "no," then use labeling to uncover the reasons behind resistance.
  3. Seek collaboration via open-ended questions, which start with "How" or "What" (instead of "Why") and phrased in a tone of seeking advice.
